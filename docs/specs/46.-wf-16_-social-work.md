# VistA Evolved
Wireframe Specifications
WF-16: Social Work
Document 16 of 16 | 6 Pages | Social Work Dashboard, Discharge Planning, Homeless Veteran Programs, Caregiver Support, Community Resource Referrals, Advance Directives & Crisis Intervention
March 2026 | The Social Work module provides case management, discharge planning, homeless veteran program coordination, caregiver support program management (PCAFC/PGCSS), community resource referral tracking, advance directive documentation, and crisis intervention support. VA Social Work is among the largest social work employers in the United States. Social workers serve as essential members of PACT (Patient Aligned Care Teams), inpatient care teams, emergency department teams, homeless veteran outreach teams, and specialty care teams. Unlike most VistA packages, Social Work does not have a dedicated VistA application file system. Social workers document through: TIU Progress Notes (#8925) using SW-specific document classes, Health Factors for screening documentation (PHQ-2/PHQ-9, AUDIT-C, MST screening, homelessness screening, food insecurity screening), Consults (#123) for social work referrals, and PCE encounters for workload credit. VistA Evolved creates DEDICATED social work data structures that do not exist in native VistA, providing structured case management, referral tracking, and program coordination capabilities that previously required external tools (spreadsheets, SharePoint, paper logs). This module is accessible as a sub-module within the Clinical Workspace (WF-02 Section 8) or as standalone pages for Social Work service-level management. Used by: Licensed Clinical Social Workers (LCSWs), Social Work Case Managers, Discharge Planners, Homeless Program Coordinators, Caregiver Support Coordinators (CSCs), Social Work Supervisors. Cross-references WF-02 Clinical Workspace pages SW-01 through SW-06.

# 1. Social Work Navigation Structure
Social Work pages are accessible in TWO contexts: (A) PATIENT CONTEXT (within Clinical Workspace WF-02): when a patient is selected, social work pages show patient-specific data (this patient's discharge plan, this patient's referrals, this patient's caregiver support enrollment). The Patient Banner IS displayed. (B) SERVICE-LEVEL CONTEXT (standalone or via Admin view): no specific patient selected, social work pages show caseload management views (all patients assigned to this social worker, all open discharge plans, all homeless program enrollments). Uses HORIZONTAL NESTED TABS: Dashboard, Discharge, Homeless, Caregiver, Resources, Advance Directives.

SOCIAL WORK DOCUMENTATION: social workers produce some of the longest and most complex clinical notes in the VA system. A comprehensive psychosocial assessment note can be 3-5 pages. Social work notes use SW-specific TIU document classes (Social Work Note, Discharge Planning Note, Psychosocial Assessment, Homeless Screening Note, Caregiver Assessment Note, etc.) that appear in the patient's clinical chart (CL-10 Progress Notes from WF-02). The structured data captured on the pages below SUPPLEMENTS (does not replace) the narrative TIU note.

Unique Behaviors
CASE ASSIGNMENT: unlike physician care where the system auto-assigns patients to providers via PCMM team assignment, social work cases are ASSIGNED MANUALLY by the SW supervisor or self-assigned by the social worker when accepting a consult. The caseload count per social worker is visible to the supervisor for workload balancing. Recommended caseload: 25-35 active cases per social worker for inpatient, 75-100 for outpatient (varies by facility and complexity). CONSULT TRIAGE: the SW supervisor reviews incoming consults and routes to the appropriate specialized social worker: homeless consults to HCHV/HUD-VASH social worker, discharge planning consults to inpatient social worker, caregiver consults to Caregiver Support Coordinator, crisis consults to nearest available LCSW. AUTO-GENERATED TASKS: the system creates follow-up tasks automatically from workflow actions. Example: when a discharge plan is created (SW-02) with a target discharge date, the system generates tasks at: -7 days ('Verify disposition arrangements'), -3 days ('Confirm DME delivery scheduled'), -1 day ('Final discharge readiness check'), +3 days post-discharge ('Post-discharge follow-up call'). This prevents patients from 'falling through the cracks' after discharge. SCREENING INTEGRATION: VHA mandates several screening assessments that social workers are responsible for addressing positive screens: MST (Military Sexual Trauma: positive screen triggers SW referral), Homelessness (positive screen triggers homeless program evaluation), Food Insecurity (positive screen triggers community resource referral), Intimate Partner Violence (positive screen triggers safety assessment). Positive screening results (from Health Factors documented in clinical encounters) auto-generate SW consults for follow-up.

Structured discharge plan form: (A) NEEDS ASSESSMENT: Living Situation (lives alone / with spouse / with family / homeless / group home / nursing facility — PRIOR to admission), Support System (family/caregiver availability, emergency contact, next of kin), Functional Status (from Nursing Assessment NUR-02: ADL independence level, mobility, cognition), Clinical Complexity (from clinical team: ongoing IV therapy, wound care, O2, tube feeding, dialysis — each affects disposition options). (B) DISPOSITION PLANNING: Planned Disposition (dropdown of options above), Facility Name (if transferring to SNF/LTAC/rehab: specific facility name, address, bed availability check date, acceptance confirmed Y/N, insurance authorization obtained Y/N for non-VA facility), Home Modification Needs (if home: ramp, grab bars, hospital bed, raised toilet seat — links to Prosthetics PR-05 HISA and PR-01 DME), Home Health Services (if home: skilled nursing visits, physical therapy, occupational therapy, wound care, infusion services — community or VA Home Based Primary Care), Caregiver Assessment (if home: is there a caregiver? Caregiver training needs? Link to SW-04 Caregiver Support). (C) DISCHARGE CHECKLIST: structured checklist of items that must be completed before discharge: Follow-up appointment scheduled (within 7 days for high-risk, 14 days for routine — link to Scheduling SC-01), Medications reconciled (from Pharmacy reconciliation OP-07 or equivalent), Discharge medications obtained (sent to pharmacy, patient has them), DME ordered and delivered (from Prosthetics PR-01), Home health referral placed (consult to Community Care or VA HBPC), Patient/caregiver education completed (disease management, medication management, when to call/return to ED), Transportation arranged (VA beneficiary travel, family, ambulance transfer for facility-to-facility), Advance Directive status confirmed (from SW-06). (D) POST-DISCHARGE FOLLOW-UP: follow-up call scheduled (3 days, 14 days, 30 days post-discharge), assigned follow-up SW, readmission risk score (from clinical risk model — high-risk patients get intensified follow-up). Per SAIL: 30-day readmission rate is a SAIL quality metric. Effective discharge planning directly reduces readmissions.
Layout — Timeline Panel (bottom, 120px)
Visual timeline showing admission date -> current day (highlighted) -> target discharge date. Milestones plotted: SW consult received, initial assessment completed, family meeting held, disposition confirmed, checklist items completed. Shows which milestones are on track vs behind schedule.
Data Source
PATIENT MOVEMENT #405 (admission/discharge status, ward/room), TIU #8925 (discharge planning notes), REQUEST/CONSULTATION #123 (SW consult), Nursing Assessment NUR-02 (functional status), Pharmacy (medication reconciliation), Scheduling #44 (follow-up appointments), Prosthetics (DME orders), Community Care (home health referrals), VistA Evolved discharge plan data structures (VE-specific: structured checklist, disposition tracking, post-discharge follow-up) — via ZVE SW DISCHARGE wrapper.
Unique Behaviors
DISCHARGE PLANNING BEGINS AT ADMISSION: per Joint Commission and VHA policy, discharge planning should begin within 24 hours of admission. The system auto-generates a discharge planning consult to Social Work for EVERY inpatient admission (configurable: all admissions, or only admissions meeting criteria: age >65, homeless, lives alone, complex medical, mental health, substance abuse). READMISSION PREVENTION: the post-discharge follow-up structure (3-day/14-day/30-day calls) is specifically designed to catch problems early before they result in ED visits or readmissions. The 3-day call verifies: patient arrived home/facility safely, medications obtained, understands follow-up plan, no acute issues. The 14-day call verifies: follow-up appointment attended, medications being taken, no clinical deterioration. The 30-day call: comprehensive check-in at the SAIL readmission measurement window boundary. FACILITY-TO-FACILITY TRANSFER: when disposition is SNF or LTAC, the discharge planner must: identify accepting facilities (based on patient's insurance, location preference, clinical needs), verify bed availability, obtain clinical acceptance (facility reviews patient's clinical summary and agrees to accept), arrange transportation, prepare transfer documentation. This multi-step process is tracked in the Discharge Checklist with timestamps and status per step. VA COMMUNITY NURSING HOME PROGRAM: VA contracts with community nursing homes for veterans requiring skilled nursing care. The discharge planner coordinates placement through the VA GEC (Geriatrics and Extended Care) program. Bed availability and contract status tracked in the system.

Card 1: HUD-VASH (Housing and Urban Development - VA Supportive Housing): active enrollees + available vouchers + pending applications. HUD-VASH provides Section 8 housing vouchers paired with VA case management — the single most effective program for ending veteran homelessness. Filter: Program, Housing Status, Case Manager, VI-SPDAT priority (High 8+, Medium 4-7, Low 0-3). Sort: default by VI-SPDAT (highest priority first).
Layout — Veteran Detail Panel (right 45%, on row selection)
Selected veteran's homeless program record: Demographics, Military Service (era, branch, discharge type — Other Than Honorable discharge veterans have limited VA eligibility but can access homeless services), Homeless History (chronically homeless Y/N — per HUD definition: homeless 12+ continuous months OR 4+ homeless episodes in 3 years totaling 12+ months), VI-SPDAT assessment with date and individual component scores, Barriers to Housing (income, credit, criminal history, substance use, mental health, physical disability, eviction history, documentation — each documented), Housing Goal (type of housing sought, preferred location/neighborhood, accessibility needs), Housing Search Log (properties viewed, landlord contacts, applications submitted, outcomes — accepted/denied with reason), Income Sources (VA disability compensation, Social Security, employment, VASH voucher amount), Services Provided (healthcare enrollment, mental health treatment, substance abuse treatment, legal services, employment services, benefits assistance, identification documents). Timeline: visual timeline from program entry to housing placement showing key milestones (entry -> assessment -> housing search -> housing identified -> move-in -> stabilization -> program exit with permanent housing).
Data Source
VistA Evolved homeless program data structures (VE-specific — native VistA has no dedicated homeless case management package), cross-referenced with: PATIENT #2 (demographics, military service), Clinical Reminders (homelessness screening Health Factors), TIU #8925 (SW homeless program notes), external system integration: HMIS (Homeless Management Information System) data exchange for HUD reporting, HOMES (Homeless Operations Management and Evaluation System) VA national tracking.
Unique Behaviors
VistA Evolved ADDITION: native VistA has NO dedicated homeless program management functionality. VA homeless programs historically use a combination of: spreadsheets for caseload tracking, CPRS notes for clinical documentation, HOMES (VA national system) for HUD reporting, and HMIS (community system) for CoC (Continuum of Care) reporting. VistA Evolved consolidates this into structured case management screens. HUD-VASH is the FLAGSHIP program: per VA, HUD-VASH has helped house over 100,000 homeless veterans since 2008. The program pairs HUD Section 8 Housing Choice Vouchers (rental subsidy) with VA clinical case management (mental health, substance abuse, primary care, benefits assistance). Veterans hold their voucher as long as they remain enrolled and participating. The VistA Evolved system tracks the complete HUD-VASH journey: referral -> eligibility screening -> voucher issuance -> housing search -> lease-up -> ongoing case management -> annual recertification. BY-NAME LIST: VA and HUD use a 'By-Name List' methodology: every homeless veteran in the community is known by name and their status tracked individually until housed. The SW-03 worklist IS the facility's by-name list — every literally homeless veteran should appear here with their current status and next action. This replaces the aggregate 'count of homeless veterans' approach with individualized accountability. CHRONIC HOMELESSNESS TARGETING: per HUD policy, chronically homeless veterans (longest homeless + most vulnerable) are prioritized for permanent supportive housing (HUD-VASH). The VI-SPDAT score helps triage: highest VI-SPDAT scores get housing resources first. This is a HOUSING FIRST approach — provide housing immediately, then address behavioral health and other issues from a stable housing base, rather than requiring sobriety/treatment compliance before housing.

Data Source
VistA Evolved caregiver program data structures (VE-specific: enrollment, wellness contacts, stipend tracking, application processing). Cross-referenced with: PATIENT #2 (veteran demographics, SC rating), NEW PERSON #200 (caregiver if also VA-connected), TIU #8925 (caregiver assessment notes, wellness contact notes), external system: Caregiver Record Management Application (CARMA — VA's national PCAFC management system). VistA Evolved provides the LOCAL clinical workflow; CARMA provides NATIONAL eligibility determination and stipend administration.
Unique Behaviors
PCAFC is a GROWING PROGRAM: expanded to all service eras on October 1, 2022 (previously limited to post-9/11 veterans). Currently serves approximately 35,000+ primary family caregivers nationally per VA reports. The program provides substantial benefits: monthly stipend (ranging from approximately $1,900 to $3,100+ depending on stipend level and geographic area), CHAMPVA health insurance for the caregiver, respite care, mental health counseling. LEGACY COHORT PROTECTION: per federal regulation final rule published 2025, legacy participants (those enrolled before October 1, 2020) are protected from discharge based on reassessment through September 30, 2028. The system tracks legacy vs non-legacy status to ensure compliance with this regulatory protection. CARMA INTEGRATION: VA's national CARMA system is the system of record for PCAFC eligibility determinations and stipend payments. VistA Evolved's SW-04 provides the LOCAL CLINICAL WORKFLOW — the CSC conducts wellness contacts, documents caregiver concerns, coordinates respite care, and communicates with the PACT team through VistA Evolved. Eligibility determinations and stipend administration flow through CARMA. Data exchange between VistA Evolved and CARMA ensures consistency. WELLNESS CONTACTS ARE CLINICAL ENCOUNTERS: per PCAFC redesign, wellness contacts are structured clinical encounters (not just administrative check-ins) that assess: caregiver physical and mental health (burnout, depression, role strain), veteran's clinical stability, caregiver training needs, service adequacy, any safety concerns. Positive findings (caregiver in crisis, veteran's condition deteriorating) trigger immediate action: respite care authorization, mental health referral for caregiver, PACT team notification of clinical change.

Unique Behaviors
SOCIAL DETERMINANTS OF HEALTH (SDOH): community resource referrals address Social Determinants of Health — the non-clinical factors that profoundly impact health outcomes: housing stability, food security, financial stability, transportation access, social support, neighborhood safety. VA has increasingly recognized that medical treatment alone cannot achieve optimal outcomes if patients lack stable housing, adequate nutrition, or financial resources. The referral tracking system provides structured accountability for SDOH interventions. WHOLE HEALTH INTEGRATION: VA's Whole Health model emphasizes care beyond clinical treatment — including connection to meaning and purpose, spirit and soul, surroundings, personal development. Community resource referrals for employment, education, social connection, and spiritual support align with Whole Health goals. REFERRAL OUTCOME TRACKING is the KEY VALUE: most healthcare systems make referrals but don't systematically track whether the patient actually connected with the resource and received help. The follow-up mechanism (follow-up date + task generation) ensures that the social worker checks back: 'Did you make it to the food bank? Were you able to enroll in the job training program? What barriers did you encounter?' This closed-loop tracking converts referrals from 'suggestions' into 'coordinated services.' RESOURCE DIRECTORY CURRENCY is a challenge: community resources change frequently (organizations lose funding, change locations, modify eligibility criteria). Stale directory entries waste patient and social worker time. The system tracks 'Last Verified Date' and flags entries not verified within 12 months for re-verification. Crowdsourced updates: any social worker who discovers changed information about a resource can submit an update, subject to supervisor verification before publication.

Per episode: Date, Type (Suicidal Ideation / Suicidal Attempt / Homicidal Ideation / Domestic Violence / Sexual Assault / Acute Psychotic Episode / Substance Intoxication-OD / Self-Harm / Other Safety Concern), Presenting Situation (brief narrative), Assessment Performed (Columbia Suicide Severity Rating Scale C-SSRS score if SI/SA, safety assessment results), Interventions (Veteran Crisis Line referral, safety planning completed, lethal means counseling, 1:1 observation initiated, emergency department referral, involuntary hold if applicable), Safety Plan (if applicable: link to safety plan document in TIU — per VA Suicide Prevention: every veteran with SI should have a documented safety plan), Disposition (patient safe — follow-up scheduled / patient transferred to ED / patient admitted to inpatient psychiatry / patient left AMA with safety concerns documented), Follow-Up (scheduled within 24 hours for SI/SA, within 7 days for other crises). NEW CRISIS ENTRY: form for documenting a new crisis intervention episode with all fields above. SAFETY PLAN VIEWER: if a safety plan exists (TIU document class: Safety Plan), displays the plan content: warning signs, internal coping strategies, people/social settings that provide distraction, people the veteran can ask for help, professionals/agencies to contact in crisis, making the environment safe (lethal means). Per VA Zero Suicide Initiative: safety planning is evidence-based suicide prevention intervention.

Unique Behaviors
ADVANCE DIRECTIVE VISIBILITY: the advance directive status is displayed on the PATIENT BANNER (WF-01 Section 2) for ALL clinical staff in ALL workspaces. This is critical because: in an emergency, the care team needs to know IMMEDIATELY whether the patient has a DNR or other treatment limitations. Having the AD status on the Patient Banner means: the surgeon sees it before pre-op, the ED physician sees it during resuscitation decisions, the ICU team sees it during critical care management. Per Joint Commission Standard RI.01.05.01: 'The hospital respects the patient's right to participate in decisions about his or her care, treatment, and services.' CRISIS INTERVENTION AUDIT TRAIL: every crisis intervention episode creates a COMPREHENSIVE audit record linking: the social worker's assessment, the intervention actions taken, the disposition, and the follow-up plan. For suicide-related episodes: this documentation is reviewed by the Suicide Prevention Coordinator (SPC) as part of the facility's suicide prevention program per VHA Directive 2024-02 (Suicide Prevention Program). The SPC reviews ALL suicide-related crisis events for: (1) quality of risk assessment, (2) appropriateness of intervention, (3) follow-up compliance, (4) system-level learning (were there warning signs that could have been detected earlier?). VETERAN CRISIS LINE INTEGRATION: for patients in active suicidal crisis, the system provides a ONE-CLICK action to initiate a warm transfer to the Veterans Crisis Line (1-800-273-8255, Press 1). The warm transfer: social worker stays on the line while the VCL crisis counselor joins, provides clinical handoff (patient name, current situation, safety assessment results), and remains available while VCL provides crisis intervention. The VCL contact is logged in the crisis intervention record. LETHAL MEANS COUNSELING: per VA/DoD Clinical Practice Guideline for Assessment and Management of Patients at Risk for Suicide, lethal means counseling (discussing safe storage of firearms, medications, and other means) is a standard component of safety planning. The crisis intervention form includes a lethal means counseling section: means identified in the environment, counseling provided, voluntary means restriction agreed (patient agreed to lock firearms, remove medications from bedside, etc.), outcome. PATIENT RECORD FLAG: for veterans identified as HIGH suicide risk, a Patient Record Flag (behavioral flag from PT-11) is applied. This flag displays a prominent banner when ANY user opens this patient's chart in ANY workspace: 'HIGH RISK FOR SUICIDE — Review Safety Plan — Follow Suicide Prevention Protocol.' This ensures every clinician who encounters this patient is aware of the elevated risk and can implement appropriate safety precautions.
